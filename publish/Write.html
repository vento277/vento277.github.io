The Need to Write

April 2024


Most people are encouraged to read as kids, but it almost never sticks. Before coming to Canada, I rarely read either. When I did, I was engrossed in Korean poetry, captivated by its expression of emotions and sceneries.


When I landed in Vancouver at the age of 16, I found myself placed in the lowest ESL (English as a Second Language) class at my high school. This meant I would most likely struggle through my secondary education. But it wasn’t the forthcoming struggles that bothered me. Being at the bottom of the barrel troubled me more than anything else.


To familiarize myself with English, I started reading books for kindergarten students. I still remember the look of a girl in the library when I walked up to her aisle. It was a mix of wonder and confusion. She probably mistook me for a reading buddy, while I felt terribly embarrassed to be there. Funnily enough, we quickly became friends. She talked about the things she liked, which of her peers annoyed her, and why she hated math so much, while I practiced my listening and speaking skills. She remains one of the best English teachers I've ever had.


Fast-forward to my freshman year of college, I grew somewhat detached from studying English. My degree in engineering didn’t demand extreme proficiency in it. Nonetheless, I continued to read books, mainly on love and investments. Then, I stumbled upon the works of Paul Graham.


Before encountering his work, I hadn’t thought of reading essays. For me, essays were assignments that had to be done for school, not something to be read leisurely. He changed that. They were compact, filled with ideas and reasoning. It made me think that most books were wasting words and, therefore, time.


As I read more essays, I grew interested in writing my own. Ideas would appear in my head, seemingly perfect in their chain of reasoning, only to be found flawed when written down. What also seemed easy at first was the process of choosing a topic. It turns out most, if not all, of my ideas were either from my parents, school, or entertainment I chose to consume. I rarely pursued a thought in its pure form, tapping the branches of possible answers. Doing this for the first time felt like playing a game of chess, but without any rows or columns.


In other words, my mind was untrained. Thoughts were like an overnight dream, clouded by the spontaneity of the mind. Only in writing them down did I discover what it is like to truly give shape to otherwise random one-liners soon to be forgotten. Writing allowed me to stress-test and examine the chain of thought, getting closer to completeness. Of course, completeness does not mean it is correct, but at least the causal effect exists so that an idea is not randomly dispersed.


The need to read is prevalent among parents and kids. The need to write, on the other hand, is not as enforced. This is likely because most adults don’t write, at least not their thoughts or opinions. When is the last time you’ve seen a political opinion written down in detail by someone close to you? And yet, when the topic comes up, most have a say about it. We trust the spontaneity of the mind too much. I would argue that most people's so-called opinions are just that.


As I go through my early 20s, the importance of writing seems more crucial than ever. It is arguably the most fundamental way of expressing one’s mind, and it is also the best tool to train a mind to think critically about matters that are not entirely numerical.


Most minds are capable of critical thinking, but all have their flaws. I aim to use writing as a tool to bypass spontaneous opinions and thoughts on topics I truly care about. Not writing about the topics I care about means I would never have a chance to form complete opinions on them. Therefore, I have a need to write, at least, about topics that I think are important.
