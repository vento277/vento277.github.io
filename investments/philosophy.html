INVESTMENT PHILOSOPHY


See’s Candy - it was acquired at premium over book value and it worked. Hochschild-Kohn, the department store chain, was bought at a discount from book and liquidating value. It didn’t work. Those two things together helped shift our thinking to the idea of paying higher prices for better businesses. 


- Charlie Munger


I see investing in common stocks as a way to build a personal holding company that aims to be a permanent owner of the best businesses around the world. This approach naturally results in a portfolio with minimal turnover.


Market

The stock market streamlines the process of price negotiation. Unlike private M&As, where customized deals can result in more efficient pricing, the public market sometimes offers buyers irrationally priced opportunities. Furthermore, the market provides access to shares of companies whose qualities far exceed the norm, allowing investors to capture both price inefficiencies and buy high-quality companies at a fair price.


Valuation

Valuing a company involves predicting its future cash flow yields based on qualitative insights and the estimated risk-free rate, using worst-case assumptions to ensure a meaningful margin of safety.


As such, the essence of business valuation lies in qualitative aspects such as barriers to entry, pricing power, and other fundamental elements that underpin a company’s moat. Quantitative information, such as financials, provides a structured framework to support and validate these qualitative assessments. Therefore, a meaningful valuation of a business can only be achieved with in-depth industry knowledge, as meaningful qualitative assessments cannot be made without it.


Risk

The risk of an investment isn't defined by a share's volatility but by the performance of the underlying business relative to the price paid for its shares. Assessing risk involves determining the degree of certainty regarding the business's long-term economics and the management team’s virtue. With keen judgment in these areas, the spread between anticipated and actual future cash flows can be minimized. Therefore, managing risk is a natural derivative of sound valuation for high-quality businesses.


Diversification

Diversification is meaningful when it considers the correlations between sectors to avoid simply replicating a market index. Over-diversification leads to average (indexed) performance, which defeats the purpose of investing in individual companies.


In my view, any idea beyond the 10th best isn't worth pursuing for a good risk-return profile. Why play a high-card when the game goes on indefinitely and the deck is nearly unlimited? Why not bet big on straights or full houses? Owning a select few - between 3 to 10 - exceptional businesses is enough to manage risk while achieving above-average returns.


Acquisition Criteria

  • Understandable

  • Consistent earning power - revenue growth of 10% or more

  • Good returns on equity with little to no debt

  • Competent management with an excellent track record


Steps to buy:

  1. Determine if the company is within the circle of competence.

  2. Develop a thesis and derive its intrinsic value.

  3. Check the market price. If the market offers them below the calculated price, review the analysis to ensure nothing was missed.

  4. Compare the opportunity cost with the current holdings. If the potential gain is substantial compared to an investment, or holding cash, invest. If not, move on.


When to sell:

  • Lose confidence in the management team. (100%)

  • Initial investment thesis doesn’t play out in 2~5 years. (100%)

  • Economics of the business changes - the business is no longer exceptional. (100%)

  • The market offers a chance to sell shares of an exceptional business at a price far above their intrinsic value when there is a new idea to take advantage of. (10~30%)
