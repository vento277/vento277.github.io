INVESTMENT PHILOSOPHY


I think of investing in common stocks as a way to build a personal holding company, treating public shares like an ownership stake in a local private business. Just as you wouldn’t frequently trade ownership in a private company, I consider these shares as long-term investments. This approach naturally leads to a portfolio with a low turnover rate.


Market

The stock market is a tool that streamlines price negotiations. Unlike private M&As, where customized deals often lead to more efficient pricing, the public market sometimes presents buyers with irrationally priced opportunities. The market also allows buyers to wait for the right price or walk away with minimal impact on the deal.


Valuation

The essence of business valuation lies in qualitative aspects such as barriers to entry, pricing power, and other fundamental elements that underpin a company’s moat. Quantitative information, such as financials, provides a structured framework to support and validate these qualitative assessments. Therefore, a meaningful valuation of a business can only be achieved with in-depth industry knowledge, as meaningful qualitative assessments cannot be made without it.


Risk

Risk of an investment is not a share’s volatility but the performance of its underlying business relative to the price paid for its shares. To assess the risk, is to define the degree of certainty with regards to the long-term economics of the business and management team’s virtue. With shrewd judgment for these two factors, the spread between anticipated and the actual future streams of cash can be narrowed, which allows for a clearer view of the opportunity cost for its shares.


Diversification

Diversification is meaningful when it considers the correlations between sectors to avoid simply replicating a market index. Over-diversification leads to average (indexed) performance, which defeats the purpose of investing in individual companies.


In my view, any portfolio allocation of less than 5% is ineffective, assuming the capital managed is under billions of dollars. Owning a select few - between 3 to 12 - exceptional businesses across sufficiently uncorrelated sectors is enough to manage risk while achieving superior results.


Acquisition Criteria

  • Understandable

  • Consistent earning power - revenue growth of 10% or more

  • Good returns on equity with little to no debt

  • Competent management with an above-average track record


Steps to buy:

  1. Determine if the company is within the circle of competence.

  2. Develop a thesis and derive its intrinsic value.

  3. Check the market price. If the market offers them below the calculated price, review the analysis to ensure nothing was missed.

  4. Compare the opportunity cost with the current holdings. If the potential gain is substantial compared to an investment, or holding cash, invest. If not, move on.


When to sell:

  • Lose confidence in the management team. (sell 100%)

  • Initial investment thesis doesn’t play out in 2~5 years. (sell 80~100%)

  • Economics of the business changes - the business is no longer exceptional. (sell 80~100%)

  • The market offers a chance to sell shares at a price far above their intrinsic value. (sell 10~20%)
